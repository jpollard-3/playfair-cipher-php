The Playfair Cipher

Cipher (noun): A method of transforming text to hide its meaning - Merriam-Webster.

The Playfair Cipher was a groundbreaking development in cryptography. It was the first to encrypt pairs of letters (called digraphs) instead of single characters. Invented in 1854 by Charles Wheatstone and championed by his friend Lyon Playfair, it offered a significant step forward in secure communication.

How It Works

Before any messages are exchanged, the sender and receiver agree on a keyword. This keyword is used to construct a 5x5 grid of letters, starting with the keyword itself eliminating duplicated letters and filling in the rest of the alphabet (I and J share a space).

To encrypt, the plaintext is split into pairs of letters. If a pair contains the same letter twice or needs an extra character to complete it, an X is inserted. Each digraph is then transformed:

  • If both letters appear in the same row, they're replaced with the letters to their right.
  • If in the same column, they're replaced by the letters below.
  • If they form a rectangle, each letter is replaced by the one on the same row but in the other column.

(Decryption works the same way in reverse shifting left or up instead.)

Simple to learn, yet surprisingly effective, the Playfair Cipher marked a clever leap in 19th-century cryptographic design.
